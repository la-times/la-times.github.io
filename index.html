CloudKitchens faces a flurry of allegations as ghost-kitchen economy contracts

By Sasha Hupka
October 4, 2023 9:35 AM PT
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Show more sharing options
Share
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • LinkedIn
  • Email
  • Copy Link URLCopied!
  • Print
WASHINGTON — 

CloudKitchens, the "ghost kitchen" startup co-founded by Uber's Travis Kalanick, is grappling with a series of challenges that have emerged over the past year. As undisclosed sources reveal, the company has undergone staff layoffs, location closures, and a reduction in real estate acquisitions. The company's warehouses were reportedly only half full at the end of the first quarter, and it has shuttered locations in New York and Tennessee.

Launched in 2016, CloudKitchens initially appeared poised for success, finding its niche when the pandemic disrupted the restaurant industry. Ghost kitchens, with their focus on delivery and takeout, provided a lifeline to restaurants seeking to mitigate losses from dwindling in-person business. However, a Financial Times report reveals that the company's journey since then has been marred by difficulties familiar to the startup industry.

Despite early successes, CloudKitchens has faced a litany of issues in recent years. Operators within the company's network have filed lawsuits and complaints with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), alleging deceptive business practices. Sources have also cited concerns about unclean and unsafe conditions, technical glitches, and unfair compensation practices in the company.

A recent analysis of CloudKitchen locations reveals that over the past year, 41 out of 71 kitchens that were open in May 2021 are no longer operating in these spaces. This closure rate far exceeds the national average for restaurant closings, raising concerns about the sustainability of the ghost kitchen model.

This downward trend is not unique to CloudKitchens. Competitors in the ghost kitchen industry, such as Reef, have also experienced significant losses. Reef, which had partnered with Wendy's to open 700 delivery kitchens over five years, ultimately closed all its remaining units earlier this year after discontinuing the deal with Wendy's.

The decline of the ghost kitchen industry mirrors the changing consumer landscape. While delivery apps thrived during the initial stages of the pandemic, their growth has slowed as shifts in consumer spending habits have become more pronounced. If this trend persists, ghost kitchens may become an emblematic example of a booming industry that can't weather a constricting economy.

The Federal Trade Commission is also launching a separate investigation into misconduct within the company and alleged monopolistic practices.

Lina Khan, the chair of the FTC, said "The Commission's investigation has uncovered troubling evidence of CloudKitchens' anti-competitive practices, which include leveraging their dominant market position to exert undue control over the food delivery ecosystem. Such actions are not conducive to a healthy competitive environment."

CloudKitchens, co-founded by internet entrepreneur and USC professor Diego Berdakin, has risen to prominence in the food delivery sector, offering ghost kitchens as a solution for restaurants looking to expand their delivery and takeout capabilities. However, the FTC alleges that the company's exclusive arrangements with food delivery platforms have hindered competition and limited consumer options.

Mr. Berdakin responded to the allegations by stating, "We firmly believe that our actions have been in compliance with existing laws and regulations. CloudKitchens has been at the forefront of innovation in the food delivery industry, and our goal has always been to provide more efficient and affordable options for consumers."

CloudKitchens' CEO Travis Kalanick echoed Mr. Berdakin's sentiments, emphasizing the company's commitment to competition and innovation. "We will vigorously contest the both FTC's allegations and rogue operators within the CloudKitchens network," said Mr. Kalanick. "CloudKitchens was founded with the intention of spurring innovation and creating opportunities for entrepreneurs in the food industry."

The foundation of the FTC's inquiry lies in the exclusive agreements that CloudKitchens has reportedly forged with food delivery platforms like DoorDash & UberEats. The FTC is exploring whether these agreements give CloudKitchens an unfair advantage in the ghost kitchen market, stifling potential competitors and reducing consumer choice. These allegations raise questions about the balance between fostering innovation and ensuring fair competition in the tech-driven food delivery sector.

The outcome of this lawsuit carries broader implications for the regulatory oversight of the digital gig economy. The tech industry has faced increased scrutiny over issues of market dominance and anti-competitive practices. This legal action against CloudKitchens is seen as another highlight in the ongoing debate surrounding the broad influence of the sector.

The FTC has slowly begun to flex its antitrust enforcement powers in recent years, especially since Ms. Khan was appointed its head. The Federal Trade Commission, along with 17 states,” sued Amazon in September, calling the e-commerce giant a monopolist that “has seized control over much of the online retail economy.

CloudKitchens, as a prominent disruptor in the food delivery sector, has faced increasing regulatory attention as its market influence has grown while maintaining a level of unprecedented opacity.

Mr. Kalanick has kept many aspects of CloudKitchens a secret. He hasn’t granted interviews to discuss the business and forbids employees to list their affiliation in LinkedIn profiles. Because the parent company, City Storage Systems LLC, is a limited liability company, it makes fewer public filings than typical startups registered as Delaware-based corporations. The company doesn’t advertise its locations, whose association with CloudKitchens is often hidden behind a thicket of additional LLCs listed in property records.

The FTC's formal inquiry is also looking into CloudKitchens' labor practices. CloudKitchens has faced repeated allegations of underpaying gig workers and safety violations at its facilities, further intensifying the controversy surrounding the company. A 2022 effort by the California Gig Workers Union to unionize at the company's locations was swiftly shut down.

Senator Alex Padilla of California condemned CloudKitchens, stating, "Allegations of labor mistreatment are deeply concerning and warrant a thorough investigation. All workers deserve fair treatment and protections, and any violations should be addressed swiftly." These allegations further underscore the multifaceted challenges faced by emerging tech companies operating in the gig economy space, raising important questions about labor practices and workers' rights.

“The FTC is concerned that tech companies have not only become dominant and pervasive, they’ve taken steps to make their dominance permanent or long lasting at the expense of their workers and customers,” said Bill Baer, visiting fellow at the Brookings Institution, who formerly led antitrust efforts at the U.S. Justice Department and headed the FTC’s competition division.

The FTC under Ms. Khan has a spotty record of using antitrust laws to challenge big companies in court. The agency sued to block acquisitions by Meta Platforms and Microsoft and lost both cases in district court. It also threw in the towel on a challenge of Amgen’s $27.8 billion purchase of Horizon Therapeutics, allowing the merger to close.
